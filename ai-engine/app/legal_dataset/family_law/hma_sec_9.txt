ACT: Hindu Marriage Act, 1955
SECTION: 9
TITLE: Restitution of Conjugal Rights
CATEGORY: Family Law - Marital Remedies
---
Section 9 of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 provides that when either the husband or the wife has, without reasonable excuse, withdrawn from the society of the other, the aggrieved party may apply to the district court by petition for restitution of conjugal rights. The court, upon being satisfied of the truth of the statements made in the petition and that there is no legal ground why the application should not be granted, may decree restitution of conjugal rights accordingly. The burden of proving reasonable excuse for withdrawal from the other's society lies on the party who has withdrawn. Reasonable excuses recognized by courts include cruelty, desertion, adultery, or any other conduct making it unreasonable to expect the aggrieved party to live with the respondent. The constitutional validity of Section 9 was challenged and upheld by the Supreme Court in Saroj Rani v. Sudarshan Kumar Chadha (1984), where the court held that the provision serves a social purpose in preserving the institution of marriage and is not violative of Article 21 (right to life and personal liberty). However, the Andhra Pradesh High Court in T. Sareetha v. T. Venkata Subbaiah (1983) had declared a similar provision unconstitutional as violative of the right to privacy, though this view was not adopted by the Supreme Court. If the respondent fails to comply with the decree of restitution of conjugal rights for a period of one year, it becomes an independent ground for divorce under Section 13(1A)(ii). The decree cannot be directly enforced by physical compulsion; rather, it can be enforced through attachment of property and is primarily used as a precursor to claiming divorce on the ground of non-compliance.
