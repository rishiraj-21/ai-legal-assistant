ACT: Guardians and Wards Act, 1890
SECTION: Overview
TITLE: Guardians and Wards Act 1890 - Comprehensive Overview
CATEGORY: Family Law - Guardianship and Custody
---
The Guardians and Wards Act, 1890 is a secular legislation that governs the appointment and powers of guardians for the person and property of minors in India, applicable to all citizens regardless of religion. Section 7 empowers the court to appoint a guardian of the person or property or both of a minor, and Section 9 provides that the court may make such order as it thinks fit regarding the custody and education of the minor. The paramount consideration in all matters relating to guardianship and custody is the "welfare of the minor" as mandated by Section 17, which requires the court to consider the minor's age, sex, religion, the character and capacity of the proposed guardian, the wishes of the deceased parent, and any existing or previous relationship with the proposed guardian. The father is generally considered the natural guardian of a minor during the father's lifetime under Hindu law (Section 6, Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956), but the Supreme Court in Githa Hariharan v. Reserve Bank of India (1999) held that the mother can also act as natural guardian during the father's lifetime. In custody disputes between parents, courts apply the welfare principle, and the tender years doctrine gives preference to the mother for custody of children below five years of age, though this is not an inflexible rule. Section 25 provides for the removal of a guardian if the guardian abuses the trust, is incapable of performing duties, neglects the ward, or acts against the ward's interest. The court can also appoint a guardian ad litem for the purpose of specific legal proceedings. Under Section 19, no guardian can be appointed for a minor who is married if the court considers the minor's spouse fit to be the guardian. The Act works in conjunction with personal laws governing guardianship and custody for different religious communities.
