ACT: Hindu Marriage Act, 1955
SECTION: 13
TITLE: Divorce
CATEGORY: Family Law - Marriage and Divorce
---
Section 13 of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 enumerates the grounds on which a decree of divorce may be obtained by either spouse. The grounds available to both husband and wife include: voluntary sexual intercourse with any person other than the spouse (adultery), cruelty of such a nature that it causes a reasonable apprehension that it will be harmful or injurious for the petitioner to live with the respondent, desertion for a continuous period of not less than two years, conversion to another religion, unsoundness of mind or mental disorder of such a kind and to such an extent that the petitioner cannot reasonably be expected to live with the respondent, virulent and incurable form of leprosy, venereal disease in communicable form, renunciation of the world by entering any religious order, and not being heard of as being alive for a period of seven years or more. Section 13(2) provides additional grounds exclusively for the wife, including: the husband has been guilty of rape, sodomy, or bestiality, or a decree or order of maintenance has been passed against the husband under Section 125 CrPC and cohabitation has not been resumed for one year or more. Section 13B, inserted by the 1976 amendment, provides for divorce by mutual consent after one year of separation. The Supreme Court in Amardeep Singh v. Harveen Kaur (2017) held that the six-month cooling period under Section 13B(2) can be waived in appropriate cases. The concept of "irretrievable breakdown of marriage" has been applied by the Supreme Court as a ground for exercising its powers under Article 142 of the Constitution to grant divorce, even though it is not a statutory ground under Section 13. The Supreme Court in Shilpa Sailesh v. Varun Sreenivasan (2023) expanded the exercise of powers under Article 142 for dissolution of marriage.
