ACT: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908
SECTION: 151
TITLE: Saving of Inherent Powers of Court
CATEGORY: Civil Procedure - Inherent Powers
---
Section 151 of the Code of Civil Procedure provides that nothing in the Code shall be deemed to limit or otherwise affect the inherent power of the court to make such orders as may be necessary for the ends of justice or to prevent abuse of the process of the court. This section preserves the residuary power of the civil court that is inherent in its very constitution as a court of law. The inherent power under Section 151 can be exercised only when there is no specific provision in the CPC dealing with the matter, as it cannot override express statutory provisions. The Supreme Court in Manohar Lal Chopra v. Rai Bahadur Rao Raja Seth Hiralal (1962) held that inherent powers should be exercised sparingly and with caution, only in exceptional circumstances where the interests of justice demand intervention. Common applications include: recalling orders passed without jurisdiction, setting aside proceedings vitiated by fraud, staying suits in appropriate circumstances, and allowing amendment of pleadings in the interest of justice when the formal provision does not cover the situation. The inherent power cannot be used to do what is prohibited by the Code, nor to bypass the conditions laid down by other provisions. In Ram Chand and Sons Sugar Mills v. Kanhayalal (1966), the Supreme Court clarified that inherent powers should not be used as a substitute for specific remedies available under the Code. Section 151 serves as a safety valve to ensure that no injustice is perpetrated by rigid application of procedural law, reflecting the principle that procedure is the handmaid of justice, not its mistress.
