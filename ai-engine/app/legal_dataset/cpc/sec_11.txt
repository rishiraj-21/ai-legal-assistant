ACT: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908
SECTION: 11
TITLE: Res Judicata
CATEGORY: Civil Procedure - Jurisdiction and Bars
---
Section 11 of the Code of Civil Procedure embodies the doctrine of res judicata, which means "a matter adjudged." It provides that no court shall try any suit or issue in which the matter directly and substantially in issue has been directly and substantially in issue in a former suit between the same parties, or between parties under whom they or any of them claim, litigating under the same title, in a court competent to try such subsequent suit, and has been heard and finally decided by such court. The essential conditions for the application of res judicata are: the matter must have been directly and substantially in issue in the former suit, the former suit must have been between the same parties or their privies, the parties must have litigated under the same title, the court that decided the former suit must have been competent to try the subsequent suit, and the matter must have been heard and finally decided. The doctrine is based on the principles of public policy (interest reipublicae ut sit finis litium — it is in the interest of the state that there should be an end to litigation) and private justice (nemo debet bis vexari pro una et eadem causa — no person should be vexed twice for the same cause). The Supreme Court in Satyadhyan Ghosal v. Deorajin Debi (1960) explained that res judicata applies equally to issues within a suit (constructive res judicata under Explanation IV). Constructive res judicata bars a party from raising any ground of attack or defence which was available to them in the former suit but was not raised. The doctrine applies to all civil proceedings and has been extended to writ proceedings and certain criminal proceedings by judicial interpretation.
