ACT: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908
SECTION: 9
TITLE: Courts to Try All Civil Suits Unless Barred
CATEGORY: Civil Procedure - Jurisdiction
---
Section 9 of the Code of Civil Procedure establishes that the courts shall have jurisdiction to try all suits of a civil nature excepting suits of which their cognizance is either expressly or impliedly barred. A suit is of a civil nature if it involves a right to property or a right to an office, or if it raises questions of civil rights and obligations. The presumption under Section 9 is in favour of jurisdiction — the court has jurisdiction unless it is expressly or impliedly excluded by any enactment. The burden of proving that the court's jurisdiction is barred lies on the party asserting such bar. The Supreme Court in Dhulabhai v. State of Madhya Pradesh (1968) laid down comprehensive principles for determining when civil court jurisdiction is excluded: exclusion must be explicit or by necessary implication, even if jurisdiction is excluded, the civil court can examine whether the provisions of the special act have been complied with, and the civil court always has jurisdiction to examine whether the tribunal acted within its powers. A suit for specific performance of contract, damages for breach of contract, declaration of title, partition of property, injunction against trespass, and recovery of money are all examples of suits of civil nature. Even where a special tribunal is created, if the remedy provided is not adequate or effective, the civil court's jurisdiction is not barred. Section 9 is a foundational provision ensuring that no person is denied access to civil justice except by clear legislative mandate.
