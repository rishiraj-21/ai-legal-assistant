ACT: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908
SECTION: Order XXXIX
TITLE: Temporary Injunctions and Interlocutory Orders
CATEGORY: Civil Procedure - Injunctions
---
Order XXXIX of the Code of Civil Procedure governs the grant of temporary injunctions and interlocutory orders to preserve the status quo during the pendency of a suit. Rule 1 provides that the court may grant a temporary injunction where it is proved that any property in dispute is in danger of being wasted, damaged, or alienated by any party, or where the defendant threatens to dispossess the plaintiff or otherwise cause injury in relation to the property. Rule 2 empowers the court to grant an injunction to restrain the defendant from committing a breach of contract or other injury, and the court may also order the act complained of to be undone. The Supreme Court in Dalpat Kumar v. Prahlad Singh (1992) laid down the three cardinal principles for granting temporary injunctions: the applicant must establish a prima facie case, the balance of convenience must be in the applicant's favour, and the applicant must show irreparable injury would be caused if the injunction is not granted. Rule 2A provides for consequences of disobedience of an injunction, including detention in civil prison for up to three months and attachment of property. Order XXXIX Rule 3 mandates that before granting an injunction, the court shall direct notice to the opposite party, except where delay would defeat the purpose, in which case an ex parte interim injunction may be granted. The court must record reasons for granting ex parte injunction and the matter must be listed within thirty days for consideration of the application on merit. Temporary injunctions are discretionary remedies, and the court exercises this discretion judicially, considering all relevant facts and circumstances of the case.
