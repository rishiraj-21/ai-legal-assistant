ACT: Indian Contract Act, 1872
SECTION: 10
TITLE: What Agreements Are Contracts
CATEGORY: Contract Law - Formation of Contract
---
Section 10 of the Indian Contract Act provides that all agreements are contracts if they are made by the free consent of parties competent to contract, for a lawful consideration and with a lawful object, and are not hereby expressly declared to be void. This section lays down the essential requirements for a valid contract under Indian law. The parties must be "competent to contract" as defined under Section 11, meaning they must be of the age of majority (18 years under the Indian Majority Act, 1875), of sound mind as per Section 12, and not disqualified from contracting by any law. Free consent, defined under Section 14, requires that consent must not be caused by coercion (Section 15), undue influence (Section 16), fraud (Section 17), misrepresentation (Section 18), or mistake (Sections 20-22). The consideration and object must be lawful under Section 23, meaning they must not be forbidden by law, defeat the provisions of any law, be fraudulent, involve injury to person or property, or be immoral or opposed to public policy. Agreements expressly declared void include agreements by incompetent persons (Section 11), agreements without consideration (Section 25), agreements in restraint of marriage (Section 26), agreements in restraint of trade (Section 27), agreements in restraint of legal proceedings (Section 28), and wagering agreements (Section 30). The Supreme Court has consistently held that Section 10 is the cornerstone of contract law in India, and all five conditions must be simultaneously satisfied for an agreement to be enforceable as a contract.
