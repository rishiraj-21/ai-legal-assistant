ACT: Indian Contract Act, 1872
SECTION: 74
TITLE: Compensation for Breach of Contract Where Penalty Stipulated
CATEGORY: Contract Law - Remedies for Breach
---
Section 74 of the Indian Contract Act provides that when a contract has been broken, if a sum is named in the contract as the amount to be paid in case of such breach, or if the contract contains any other stipulation by way of penalty, the party complaining of the breach is entitled, whether or not actual damage or loss is proved to have been caused thereby, to receive from the party who has broken the contract reasonable compensation not exceeding the amount so named or the penalty stipulated. Indian law under Section 74 does not draw the distinction between liquidated damages and penalty that exists in English law — in both cases, the court awards reasonable compensation not exceeding the stipulated amount. The Supreme Court in Fateh Chand v. Balkishan Das (1963) held that Section 74 is applicable to all stipulations by way of penalty, whether in the form of an amount named in the contract or forfeiture of a deposit, and the jurisdiction of the court is to award such compensation as it deems reasonable. In Kailash Nath Associates v. DDA (2015), the Supreme Court revisited the law and held that where the forfeiture of earnest money is reasonable (typically 10% of the total consideration), it can be forfeited, but amounts beyond that must be refunded unless the other party proves actual loss. The Explanation to Section 74 clarifies that a stipulation for increased interest from the date of default is a stipulation by way of penalty. The party in breach cannot be made to pay more than the stipulated amount, and the party claiming compensation need not prove the exact quantum of loss but must show that some loss was suffered. Section 74 applies to government contracts as well, as held by the Supreme Court in multiple decisions.
