ACT: Indian Contract Act, 1872
SECTION: 23
TITLE: What Considerations and Objects Are Lawful and What Not
CATEGORY: Contract Law - Lawful Object
---
Section 23 of the Indian Contract Act declares that the consideration or object of an agreement is unlawful if it is forbidden by law, or is of such a nature that if permitted it would defeat the provisions of any law, or is fraudulent, or involves or implies injury to the person or property of another, or the court regards it as immoral or opposed to public policy. Every agreement of which the object or consideration is unlawful is void under this section. The concept of "forbidden by law" includes acts punishable under criminal law as well as acts prohibited by statutory provisions carrying civil penalties. "Defeat the provisions of any law" covers cases where the agreement is not directly prohibited but would circumvent the purpose and policy of the law. The Supreme Court in Gherulal Parakh v. Mahadeodas Maiya (1959) held that the doctrine of public policy should be applied cautiously and should not be extended beyond its well-established boundaries. Agreements involving trading with enemy nations, agreements to commit crimes, agreements interfering with the administration of justice, agreements to defraud creditors or revenue authorities, and agreements in restraint of personal liberty are examples of agreements opposed to public policy. If any part of a single consideration for one or more objects, or any one or more of several considerations for a single object, is unlawful, the entire agreement is void under Section 24. Courts in India have held that public policy is not static and must evolve with the times, but have also cautioned against judicial activism in expanding the scope of public policy without legislative backing.
