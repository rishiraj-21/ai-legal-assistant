ACT: Indian Contract Act, 1872
SECTION: 56
TITLE: Agreement to Do Impossible Act (Frustration of Contract)
CATEGORY: Contract Law - Performance and Discharge
---
Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act deals with the doctrine of frustration of contract, also known as the doctrine of supervening impossibility. Paragraph 1 provides that an agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void. Paragraph 2 states that a contract to do an act which after the contract is made becomes impossible, or by reason of some event which the promisor could not prevent, becomes unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful. Paragraph 3 provides that where one person has promised to do something which he knew or with reasonable diligence might have known to be impossible or unlawful, he must compensate the other party for any loss sustained through the non-performance of the promise. The Indian law of frustration under Section 56 is statutory and differs from the English common law doctrine of frustration in that Indian courts apply Section 56 directly rather than implying terms. The Supreme Court in Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur (1954) held that the word "impossible" in Section 56 does not mean physical or literal impossibility but includes cases where performance becomes impracticable in the sense that the object of the parties has been frustrated. In Energy Watchdog v. CERC (2017), the Supreme Court clarified that a force majeure clause in a contract must be given effect to if it covers the event in question, and Section 56 applies only in the absence of such a clause. The COVID-19 pandemic led to extensive invocation of Section 56, with courts examining on a case-by-case basis whether the pandemic truly rendered performance impossible or merely more onerous.
