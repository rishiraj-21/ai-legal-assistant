ACT: Transfer of Property Act, 1882
SECTION: 58
TITLE: Mortgage Defined - Types of Mortgages
CATEGORY: Property Law - Mortgage
---
Section 58 of the Transfer of Property Act, 1882 defines "mortgage" as the transfer of an interest in specific immovable property for the purpose of securing the payment of money advanced or to be advanced by way of loan, an existing or future debt, or the performance of an engagement which may give rise to a pecuniary liability. The transferor is called the mortgagor, the transferee the mortgagee, the principal money and interest the mortgage money, and the instrument by which the transfer is effected the mortgage deed. Section 58 enumerates six types of mortgages: (a) Simple mortgage — where the mortgagor binds himself personally to pay the mortgage money and agrees that in default the mortgagee shall have a right to cause the property to be sold; (b) Mortgage by conditional sale — where the mortgagor ostensibly sells the property on condition that on default the sale shall become absolute or on payment the sale shall be void; (c) Usufructuary mortgage — where the mortgagor delivers possession to the mortgagee who retains it until the mortgage money is paid and receives rents and profits in lieu of interest; (d) English mortgage — where the mortgagor binds himself to repay on a certain date and transfers the property absolutely with the condition of reconveyance upon payment; (e) Mortgage by deposit of title deeds (equitable mortgage) — created by depositing title deeds with the creditor in specified towns; and (f) Anomalous mortgage — any mortgage not falling within the above categories. The right of redemption under Section 60 is an inherent right of the mortgagor which cannot be extinguished by any contract, embodying the maxim "once a mortgage, always a mortgage." Mortgage by deposit of title deeds is widely used in banking and commercial transactions for securing loans, requiring no registration but necessitating a memorandum of deposit.
