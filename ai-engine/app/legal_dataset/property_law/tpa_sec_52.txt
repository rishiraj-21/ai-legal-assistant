ACT: Transfer of Property Act, 1882
SECTION: 52
TITLE: Transfer of Property Pending Suit Relating Thereto (Lis Pendens)
CATEGORY: Property Law - Transfer Restrictions
---
Section 52 of the Transfer of Property Act, 1882 embodies the doctrine of lis pendens (litigation pending), which provides that during the pendency of a suit or proceeding in which any right to immovable property is directly and specifically in question, the property cannot be transferred or otherwise dealt with by any party to the suit or proceeding so as to affect the rights of any other party thereto under any decree or order which may be made therein, except under the authority of the court and on such terms as it may impose. The doctrine is based on the maxim "pendente lite nihil innovetur" — nothing should be changed during the pendency of litigation. The purpose is to ensure that the subject matter of litigation remains intact and available to satisfy the decree that may ultimately be passed. The Supreme Court in Bellamy v. Sabine established the principle that lis pendens is based not on the doctrine of notice (actual or constructive) but on the principle of public policy and necessity — the administration of justice would be impossible if alienations pendente lite were allowed to prevail. For Section 52 to apply, the following conditions must be met: there must be a pending suit or proceeding, the suit must be in a court of competent jurisdiction, the right to immovable property must be directly and specifically in question, the transfer must be by a party to the suit, and the transfer must affect the rights of the other party. The transferee pendente lite is bound by the decree as if he were a party to the suit, though he is not a necessary party. The 1929 amendment added the proviso allowing courts to authorize transfers during pendency on appropriate terms, providing flexibility to prevent undue hardship.
