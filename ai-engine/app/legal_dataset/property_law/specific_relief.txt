ACT: Specific Relief Act, 1963
SECTION: 12
TITLE: Specific Performance of Contract
CATEGORY: Property Law - Specific Relief
---
Section 12 of the Specific Relief Act, 1963 (as amended in 2018) governs the specific performance of contracts, which is the court-ordered actual execution of a contract according to its terms rather than merely awarding damages for breach. Prior to the 2018 amendment, specific performance was a discretionary remedy; the amendment made it the default remedy, providing that specific performance of a contract shall be enforced by the court subject to the provisions of sub-sections (2), (3), and (4). Sub-section (2) provides that the court may not grant specific performance where the act agreed to be done is such that compensation in money would be adequate relief, or where the contract is such that the court cannot supervise its performance. Section 14 specifies contracts that cannot be specifically enforced, including contracts where a party has obtained substituted performance under Section 20, contracts involving personal skill or volition, and contracts that are determinable in nature. The 2018 amendment introduced Section 14A which provides that the court shall not grant specific performance based on any ground that the court would not have granted prior to the amendment — the rationale being that specific performance should now be the norm, not the exception. Section 16 provides conditions for granting specific performance: the plaintiff must aver and prove that he has performed or has always been ready and willing to perform the essential terms of the contract. The Supreme Court in Parakunnel Alias Jacob v. Augustine (2016) held that the readiness and willingness must be shown throughout the relevant period, not just at the time of filing the suit. In immovable property transactions, specific performance is particularly significant because every piece of land is unique and damages may not adequately compensate for loss of the specific property contracted for.
