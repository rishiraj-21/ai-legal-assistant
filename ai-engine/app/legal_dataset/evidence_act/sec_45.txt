ACT: Indian Evidence Act, 1872
SECTION: 45
TITLE: Opinions of Experts
CATEGORY: Evidence Law - Expert Evidence
---
Section 45 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 provides that when the court has to form an opinion upon a point of foreign law, science, art, identity of handwriting, or finger impressions, the opinions upon that point of persons specially skilled in such foreign law, science, art, identity of handwriting, or finger impressions are relevant facts. Such persons are called "experts." The scope of expert evidence has been expanded by judicial interpretation to include DNA profiling, forensic analysis, ballistic examination, medical opinion, electronic evidence analysis, and other specialized fields. The opinion of an expert is merely advisory and not binding on the court — the court must form its own opinion after evaluating the expert evidence along with other evidence on record. The Supreme Court in Ramesh Chandra Agrawal v. Regency Hospital (2009) held that the testimony of an expert must be tested on the touchstone of acceptability, internal consistency, and corroboration with other evidence. An expert may be cross-examined like any other witness, and the weight of expert testimony depends on the expert's qualifications, experience, reasoning, and the reliability of the methodology employed. Section 45A, inserted by the Information Technology Act, 2000, specifically provides for the opinion of an Examiner of Electronic Evidence, recognizing the growing importance of digital forensics. Courts have held that where there is a conflict between expert opinions, the court must evaluate both and may accept one over the other with reasons. Expert evidence on matters of common knowledge or matters within the competence of the court is generally not admissible, as the court itself is deemed capable of forming an opinion on such matters.
