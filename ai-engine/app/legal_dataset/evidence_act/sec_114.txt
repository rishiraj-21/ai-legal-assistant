ACT: Indian Evidence Act, 1872
SECTION: 114
TITLE: Court May Presume Existence of Certain Facts
CATEGORY: Evidence Law - Presumptions
---
Section 114 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 provides that the court may presume the existence of any fact which it thinks likely to have happened, regard being had to the common course of natural events, human conduct, and public and private business, in their relation to the facts of the particular case. This section grants the court discretionary power to draw presumptions of fact based on common sense, experience, and the ordinary course of events. The Illustrations appended to the section provide important examples of permissible presumptions, including: that a man who is in possession of stolen goods soon after the theft is either the thief or has received the goods knowing them to be stolen, that an accomplice is unworthy of credit unless corroborated in material particulars, that a bill of exchange accepted or endorsed was accepted or endorsed for good consideration, that a thing or state of things which has been shown to exist within a period substantially shorter than the particular period in question continues to exist, and that judicial and official acts have been regularly performed. Unlike mandatory presumptions under Section 113A (abetment of suicide), 113B (dowry death), and 114A (absence of consent in rape), the presumptions under Section 114 are discretionary — the court "may" presume but is not bound to do so. The Supreme Court in Trimukh Maroti Kirkan v. State of Maharashtra (2006) held that the presumption under Section 114 regarding the last seen theory is a presumption of fact and not of law, and its strength depends on the proximity of time between the accused being last seen with the deceased and the discovery of the body. These presumptions are rebuttable, and the party against whom the presumption operates may lead evidence to disprove the presumed fact. The judicial application of Section 114 requires careful balancing to ensure that presumptions do not substitute for actual proof where such proof is reasonably obtainable.
