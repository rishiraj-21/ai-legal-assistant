ACT: Indian Evidence Act, 1872
SECTION: 32
TITLE: Cases in Which Statement of Relevant Fact by Person Who Is Dead or Cannot Be Found Is Relevant (Dying Declaration)
CATEGORY: Evidence Law - Hearsay Exceptions
---
Section 32 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 provides important exceptions to the rule against hearsay evidence, making statements of persons who are dead, cannot be found, have become incapable of giving evidence, or whose attendance cannot be procured without unreasonable delay or expense, admissible in certain circumstances. The most frequently invoked clause is Section 32(1), commonly known as the "dying declaration," which makes relevant a statement made by a person as to the cause of his death, or as to any of the circumstances of the transaction which resulted in his death, in cases in which the cause of that person's death comes into question. Unlike English law, Indian law does not require that the declarant was under expectation of death at the time of making the statement — the statement is admissible regardless of whether the person had any apprehension of death. The Supreme Court in Laxman v. State of Maharashtra (2002) held that a dying declaration can be the sole basis of conviction if it inspires confidence and is found to be voluntary, truthful, and not tutored. A dying declaration may be oral or written, may be recorded by a Magistrate, doctor, police officer, or any other person, and may even be in the form of gestures (as held in Queen Empress v. Abdullah). The Supreme Court in Pakala Narayana Swami v. Emperor (1939) interpreted the phrase "circumstances of the transaction" broadly to include not just the immediate cause of death but also events proximate in time and leading to the fatal transaction. Other clauses of Section 32 cover statements made in the ordinary course of business (32(2)), statements against the interest of the maker (32(3)), statements regarding public rights or customs (32(4)), statements relating to the existence of relationships (32(5)), statements in wills or deeds (32(6)), and statements by persons whose testimony is barred (32(7) and 32(8)).
