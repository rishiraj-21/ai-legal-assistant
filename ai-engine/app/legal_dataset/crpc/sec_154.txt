ACT: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973
SECTION: 154
TITLE: Information in Cognizable Cases (First Information Report)
CATEGORY: Criminal Procedure - Investigation
---
Section 154 of the Code of Criminal Procedure governs the recording of information relating to the commission of cognizable offences, commonly known as the First Information Report (FIR). Sub-section (1) provides that every information relating to the commission of a cognizable offence, if given orally to an officer in charge of a police station, shall be reduced to writing by him or under his direction and shall be read over to the informant. The informant must sign the written information, and a copy thereof shall be given to the informant free of cost. The FIR is not substantive evidence but is an important document that sets the criminal law in motion and provides the first version of the prosecution's case. The Supreme Court in Lalita Kumari v. Government of U.P. (2014) held that registration of FIR is mandatory under Section 154 when the information discloses commission of a cognizable offence, and no preliminary inquiry is permissible at that stage, except in cases involving matrimonial disputes, commercial offences, medical negligence, corruption, and cases with abnormal delay. Sub-section (3) provides that if the officer in charge of a police station refuses to record information, the informant may send the substance of the information in writing to the Superintendent of Police, who may either investigate the case himself or direct an investigation. The FIR can be filed by any person and need not be filed only by the victim or an eyewitness. Zero FIR allows registration of FIR at any police station regardless of jurisdiction, and the case is then transferred to the police station having territorial jurisdiction. Delayed FIR does not necessarily cast doubt on the prosecution's case if the delay is satisfactorily explained.
