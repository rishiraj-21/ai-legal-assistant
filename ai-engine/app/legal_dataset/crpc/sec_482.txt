ACT: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973
SECTION: 482
TITLE: Saving of Inherent Powers of High Court
CATEGORY: Criminal Procedure - Inherent Powers
---
Section 482 of the Code of Criminal Procedure preserves the inherent powers of the High Court to make such orders as may be necessary to give effect to any order under the Code, to prevent abuse of the process of any court, or to otherwise secure the ends of justice. The provision does not confer any new power but merely recognizes and preserves the pre-existing inherent jurisdiction of the High Court. The Supreme Court in State of Haryana v. Bhajan Lal (1992) laid down illustrative categories where the High Court can exercise powers under Section 482 to quash criminal proceedings, including: where the allegations do not prima facie constitute any offence, where there is no legal evidence adduced or the evidence is wholly improbable, where the criminal proceeding is manifestly attended with mala fide intention, and where the complaint suffers from a legal bar. The power under Section 482 is to be exercised sparingly, with circumspection and caution, and only when the court is convinced that there would be manifest injustice or gross abuse of process if it does not intervene. In Parbatbhai Aahir v. State of Gujarat (2017), the Supreme Court held that proceedings can be quashed when parties have amicably settled the matter, particularly in cases arising out of matrimonial and commercial disputes. The inherent power cannot be used to stifle a legitimate prosecution or to bypass the procedural requirements of the Code. Courts have also used Section 482 to transfer investigations, to direct registration of FIRs, and to ensure fair investigation in cases where the investigating agency is found to be biased.
