ACT: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973
SECTION: 164
TITLE: Recording of Confessions and Statements
CATEGORY: Criminal Procedure - Investigation
---
Section 164 of the Code of Criminal Procedure provides the procedure for recording confessions and statements before a Magistrate during the course of investigation. A confession or statement may be recorded by any Metropolitan Magistrate or Judicial Magistrate at any time during the investigation, whether or not the confessing person is in custody. Before recording a confession, the Magistrate must explain to the person making it that he is not bound to make the confession and that if he does, it may be used as evidence against him. The Magistrate must also satisfy himself that the confession is being made voluntarily, and must allow the person sufficient time (minimum 24 hours of reflection) before recording it. The recorded confession must be in the form prescribed by the State Government, and the Magistrate must make a memorandum certifying that the confession was voluntary and that the prescribed procedure was followed. The Supreme Court in Kartar Singh v. State of Punjab (1994) emphasized strict compliance with Section 164 safeguards to prevent false confessions extracted under police pressure. Statements under Section 164 (which are not confessions) are also recorded before Magistrates and serve as important evidence, particularly in cases involving vulnerable witnesses such as victims of sexual offences, children, and trafficked persons. Unlike confessions made to police officers (which are inadmissible under Section 25 of the Evidence Act), confessions recorded under Section 164 by a Magistrate are admissible as substantive evidence. The 2013 amendment mandated that statements of victims of certain sexual offences must be recorded by a Judicial Magistrate under Section 164 as soon as the commission of the offence is brought to the notice of the police.
