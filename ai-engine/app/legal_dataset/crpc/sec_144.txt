ACT: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973
SECTION: 144
TITLE: Power to Issue Order in Urgent Cases of Nuisance or Apprehended Danger
CATEGORY: Criminal Procedure - Preventive Powers
---
Section 144 of the Code of Criminal Procedure empowers a District Magistrate, Sub-Divisional Magistrate, or any Executive Magistrate specially empowered by the State Government to issue orders in urgent cases of nuisance or apprehended danger. The order may be directed to any particular individual or to persons residing in a particular place or area, requiring them to abstain from certain acts or to take certain action with respect to property in their possession or management. The order can be passed ex parte and may remain in force for up to two months from the date of the order, extendable up to six months by the State Government if it considers the prevention of danger to human life, health, or safety requires continuation. The Supreme Court in Madhu Limaye v. Sub-Divisional Magistrate (1970) held that the power under Section 144 must be exercised in emergency situations and cannot be used as a tool for routine administration or to suppress legitimate dissent. In Anuradha Bhasin v. Union of India (2020), the Supreme Court ruled that Section 144 orders must satisfy the test of proportionality and cannot be used to suppress the legitimate expression of opinion or grievance or the exercise of democratic rights. The Magistrate must apply his mind to the material facts and form an opinion about the necessity, and the order must state the material facts. Any person aggrieved by the order may challenge it before the Magistrate himself or by way of revision before the High Court. Section 144 is commonly invoked to impose curfews, restrict public gatherings, and maintain law and order during communal tensions, elections, or other volatile situations.
