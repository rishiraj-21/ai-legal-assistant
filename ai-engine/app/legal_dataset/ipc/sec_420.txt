ACT: Indian Penal Code, 1860
SECTION: 420
TITLE: Cheating and Dishonestly Inducing Delivery of Property
CATEGORY: Criminal Law - Offences Against Property
---
Section 420 of the Indian Penal Code deals with the offence of cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property. Whoever cheats and thereby dishonestly induces the person deceived to deliver any property to any person, or to make, alter, or destroy the whole or any part of a valuable security, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to seven years, and shall also be liable to fine. The offence under Section 420 is an aggravated form of cheating defined under Section 415, requiring additionally that the cheating must result in inducing delivery of property or alteration of a valuable security. The essential ingredients are: deception of any person, fraudulent or dishonest inducement to deliver property or to consent to retention, and the act must have been done intentionally from the beginning of the transaction. The Supreme Court in Hridaya Ranjan Prasad Verma v. State of Bihar (2000) held that a mere breach of contract does not constitute cheating unless fraudulent intention existed from the inception. This section is widely used in cases involving financial fraud, online scams, bogus investment schemes, real estate fraud, and identity theft. The offence is cognizable, non-bailable, and triable by a Magistrate of the first class. Courts distinguish between civil disputes arising from breach of contract and criminal fraud based on whether dishonest intention existed at the time of making the promise.
